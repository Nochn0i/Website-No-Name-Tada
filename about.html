    On Selective Attention.
    Perception is never a neutral intake of the world. It is an act of continuous, effortful triage.

    Every visual field arrives oversaturated with candidate stimuli, far more than working memory can admit at once. What the mind resolves as "seen" is therefore already the residue of an unconscious filtering process — one governed as much by relevance and threat as by contrast or motion.

    Figure 4.5 — A child's motion against a static grid isolates figure from ground with unusual clarity, illustrating pop-out effects in an otherwise low-complexity scene.

    Salience

    Consider the figure above: two pedestrians rendered small against a converging colonnade of lamp posts. The eye does not distribute itself evenly across the frame. It is drawn first to the human silhouettes, then to the vanishing point they walk toward — a reflex so automatic that most viewers cannot say why their gaze moved first, only that it did.

    This is bottom-up salience at work: features that are statistically rare within their surroundings — motion, biological form, sharp luminance edges — capture attention before any top-down goal has been formed.

    The Cost of Looking Twice.

    Attentional capture is not without expense. Each reallocation of gaze draws on a finite pool of cognitive resources, and repeated capture — by advertisement, by glare, by an unexpected figure crossing a frame — produces measurable decrements in subsequent task performance. Kahneman's early attention models treated this capacity as a single undifferentiated reservoir; contemporary accounts favor a more distributed architecture, in which distinct neural circuits compete for a shared, bottlenecked output stage. What matters pedagogically is the implication for design and environment alike: a visual field engineered for calm — evenly lit, low in unpredictable motion — measurably reduces the micro-fatigue that accumulates across a working day. Attention, in this sense, is not merely psychological. It is architectural.
